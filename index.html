Dental Assistant Award FAQ

The Practice Manager has asked me to work overtime for the last three nights and I already have plans for the fourth night. Do I have to accept the shift?

No. Under the NES, you have the right to refuse unreasonable overtime. This request is likely unreasonable because:

  • Health and Safety: Working three consecutive overtime shifts creates a clear risk of fatigue and requires proper rest.
  • Personal Commitments: The law requires consideration for your personal life, including existing plans and family responsibilities.
  • Rest Breach: The HPSS Award generally requires a 10-hour rest period between shifts after working overtime.

What do I do if a patient is flirting with me?

Always maintain professional boundaries. Politely but firmly redirect the conversation back to the dental treatment. If the behaviour continues, is inappropriate, or makes you feel uncomfortable, you must immediately report the incident to your Practice Manager or supervising dentist. Your workplace should have a clear policy on patient boundaries and harassment.

I noticed I wasn't paid overtime for one of my shifts. Should I say anything?

Yes, absolutely. Your minimum pay and entitlements, including overtime rates, are a legal guarantee under the HPSS Award. Wage underpayment is taken very seriously.

  1. Document It: Check your payslip against your shift records (start/finish times). Note the date, hours, and the amount you believe is missing.
  2. Inform Management First: Approach your Practice Manager or payroll department politely, stating that you believe there was a genuine error in calculating your hours/rate for the specific shift.
  3. Follow Up: If the issue is not resolved promptly, you can seek advice from the Fair Work Ombudsman to understand your full options for resolving the underpayment.
